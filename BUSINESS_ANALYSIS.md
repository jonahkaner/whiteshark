# Episodic Content Licensing for Short-Form Vertical Video

## Executive Summary

The micro-drama/vertical video market has exploded to **$11 billion globally in 2025**, with the US market alone generating **$1.3B**. Apps like ReelShort are making **$30M+/month** by releasing addictive 60-second episodes that users pay to unlock. The question: can you acquire old TV/movie IP cheaply and re-cut it for this format?

**Short answer: Yes, but with significant caveats. The better play may be a hybrid model.**

---

## The Market Opportunity

### Current Market Size
- **Global micro-drama market (2025):** $11 billion (Omdia)
- **US market (2025):** $1.3 billion
- **US market projected (2030):** $3.8 billion
- **Q1 2025 alone:** $700M in consumer spend across vertical drama apps
- ReelShort averaging **11M monthly active users** (50x YoY growth)

### The ReelShort Model
- 5-10 free episodes hook viewers
- Episode 6+ costs ~$0.30 or an ad view
- Users spending **$100M+/month** on coin bundles across 215 US drama apps
- Some viewers paying **$200+/month** for content
- Average daily spend per user **outperforms mid-tier mobile games**

### Production Economics
| Metric | Cost |
|--------|------|
| Production per series | $150K-$300K |
| Marketing spend | 4-5x production cost |
| Production timeline | 30 days (vs 2 years for Hollywood) |

## Content Acquisition Strategies

### Option 1: Public Domain Content (Cheapest)

**What's available:**
- All works published before 1930 are now public domain (as of 2025)
- Notable entries: Early Mickey Mouse, Popeye, classic horror films
- No licensing costs

**Challenges:**
- Limited recognizable content for modern audiences
- Visual quality issues
- Music rights often remain separate from visual rights
- Most compelling content is still under copyright

**Verdict:** Good for supplemental content, but unlikely to drive significant engagement alone.

---

### Option 2: Orphaned/Cheap Library Content

**Opportunities:**
- Studios sitting on vast libraries of underperforming titles
- Many 70s-90s TV shows have minimal streaming value
- B-movies, cancelled series, forgotten sitcoms
- Failed theatrical releases with home video rights available

**Potential Targets:**
- Roger Corman library (some already public domain)
- Syndicated TV shows with lapsed licensing
- Foreign content with expired US rights
- Direct-to-video content from 1990s-2000s

**Cost Estimates:**
- Individual film rights: $5,000 - $50,000 for lesser-known titles
- Library deals: $100K - $1M for packages of 20-50 titles
- Revenue share AVOD deals: 0 upfront, 30-50% rev share

---

### Option 3: Re-licensing "Known" Content

**Reality check:**
- MGM reports their "old" library has **"never generated as much revenue"**
- Peak TV era drove library values to all-time highs
- Major studios know the value of their catalogs

**Recent Benchmarks:**
- Lakeshore library (300 films including Million Dollar Baby): **$200M**
- Open Road assets (Spotlight, Nightcrawler): **$87.5M**
- Quality library content commands premium prices

**Verdict:** The "cheap" recognizable IP ship has largely sailed. Studios are sophisticated.

## The Real Opportunity: Hybrid Approach

### Why pure licensing may not work:

1. **Format Mismatch:** Old content wasn't shot vertically. Re-editing horizontal content loses visual information and quality.

2. **Pacing Issues:** Classic TV has 22-45 minute story structures. Modern micro-dramas are written for 60-second cliff-hangers every episode.

3. **Style Gap:** ReelShort content is described as "soap opera on cocaine" - melodramatic, fast-paced, designed for mobile. Old content often has slower pacing.

4. **The Winning Formula:** Successful micro-dramas aren't just short - they're written specifically for the format with hooks every 30-60 seconds.

### What could work:

**Strategy A: "Inspired By" Remakes**
- License story rights (cheaper than full content rights)
- Reshoot in vertical format with modern production
- Use public domain stories as source material
- Costs: $150-250K per series (same as original production)

**Strategy B: Clip + Original Hybrid**
- Use licensed archival footage as "flashbacks" or "dream sequences"
- Wrap with new vertical-native content
- Creates nostalgia hook while maintaining format integrity
- Novel differentiation from competitors

**Strategy C: Documentary/Commentary Format**
- License clips under fair use doctrine
- Create vertical "reaction" or analysis content
- Lower legal risk, lower costs
- Less engaging than narrative content

**Strategy D: FAST Channel Arbitrage**
- License content for FAST/AVOD platforms (Pluto, Tubi, etc.)
- FAST market growing but relies on ad revenue, not direct consumer spend
- Lower margins than pay-per-episode model
- Requires volume over quality

### Their Moat
- Vertical integration: own production + distribution
- Algorithm optimization for engagement
- 15+ production pods globally
- Data on what works (romance, billionaire, werewolf themes)

### Gaps You Could Exploit
- **Nostalgia arbitrage:** They focus on new IP, you could own the "classic re-imagined" niche
- **Older demographics:** Current content skews young/female; older viewers underserved
- **IP leverage:** Build on known characters vs. starting from zero brand recognition
- **True crime/documentary:** Most players focus on fiction; real stories are untapped

## Risks & Mitigations

### High Risks
1. **Platform dependency:** TikTok's US future uncertain; need multi-platform strategy
2. **Hit-driven business:** Most content fails; need volume to find winners
3. **Rights complexity:** Music, likenesses, derivative works create legal landmines
4. **Competition:** Well-funded players with production infrastructure

### Mitigations
- Build owned app/platform from day one (don't just rely on TikTok)
- Start with clearly public domain or fully-cleared content
- Partner with entertainment lawyers specializing in rights clearance
- Test concepts on social before full production

---

## Recommendations

### If you want to minimize capital:
1. Start with public domain content experiments on TikTok/YouTube Shorts
2. Test engagement with vertical re-edits of classic content
3. Build audience before spending on licensing
4. Revenue share deals for content before upfront licensing

### If you want to build a real business:
1. Raise $3-5M seed round
2. License story rights (not full content) for 10-15 recognizable properties
3. Produce original vertical content "based on" or "inspired by"
4. Build owned platform with pay-per-episode model
5. Use licensed clips as marketing hooks on social platforms

### If you want to differentiate:
1. Target underserved demographics (45+, male viewers)
2. Focus on true crime, documentary, or non-fiction vertical content
3. Create "classic TV reimagined" brand identity
4. Partner with rights holders for revenue share vs. upfront licensing
